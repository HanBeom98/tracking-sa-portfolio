Yann LeCun's $1 Billion: What's the Actual Plan?

Yann LeCun raised $1 billion? So what? While Bloomberg headlines make it sound like something huge is happening, opening the lid reveals nothing substantial. A $1 billion seed funding is certainly a significant amount, but it's just a shiny package labeled "new AI startup" without clearly stating what it intends to do with the money. Let's check the facts.

It's reasonable to see this as a collaboration between LeCun's reputation and the overheated investment sentiment in the AI industry. Frankly, it's suspicious to receive $1 billion and not disclose a specific business model or technological direction. It feels like a "let's get the money first" mentality. Of course, a figure like LeCun might be painting a 'big picture.' However, from an investor's perspective, blind investment is a no-go.

Let's look at the competition. OpenAI has dominated the market with its LLM-based ChatGPT, and Google is chasing with Gemini. Meta is expanding its ecosystem with its own LLM development and open-source strategy. In this situation, it's unclear what 'differentiating factor' LeCun's startup will use to make its move. If it simply focuses on improving LLM performance, it's jumping into a red ocean.

Some speculate that LeCun is designing a completely new architecture based on his experience at Meta. For example, to overcome the limitations of current LLMs, he could enhance RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) technology or develop new learning methods that mimic human intuitive thinking. However, this is just speculation. Unless LeCun himself presents a concrete roadmap, it's difficult to assess the value of $1 billion.

A more concerning point is that securing vast computing resources has become essential as AI semiconductor competition intensifies. The competition to secure Nvidia's GPUs is already called the 'GPU war.' Unless LeCun's startup plans to develop its own NPU (Neural Processing Unit), it will eventually have to jump into the GPU acquisition race. This will lead to astronomical expenses.

A comparison with past similar technology trends is also necessary. In the early 2010s, as machine learning technology gained prominence, numerous AI startups emerged. However, most failed due to technical limitations and a lack of business models. If LeCun's startup is to avoid following in their footsteps, it must demonstrate a clear vision and differentiated technology.

In conclusion, Yann LeCun's $1 billion seed funding is an investment in 'reputation,' but the substance is still unclear. Investors should consider it coldly. I'll be watching to see what 'magic' LeCun will perform, but it's hard to smell the money right now. It's wise to wait until more concrete information is released. Keep in mind that hasty investment can lead to blind investment.
