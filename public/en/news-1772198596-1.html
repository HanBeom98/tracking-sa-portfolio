Heineken's AI Chief: So What's the Real Game Plan?

Heineken hired an AI chief? Big deal. These days, it's almost cliché for large corporations to appoint AI executives. What matters isn't who, but what. Right now, Heineken's announcement is just an empty gesture, a shell with nothing inside.

Let's stick to the facts. Heineken has hired someone with the title "AI Chief." That's it. A concrete AI strategy? Nonexistent. What problems will they solve, what value will they create? Shrouded in mystery. It's like a startup throwing around "blockchain" to attract investment. For a giant like Heineken to sell "AI" like this is disappointing.

This kind of announcement suggests two possibilities. First, they actually intend to use AI, but haven't even properly sketched out the blueprint yet. Second, it's just a superficial gesture to show "we're doing AI too." Neither is appealing to investors.

Let's look at the competition. AB InBev is already actively using AI in various areas such as supply chain optimization and consumer behavior analysis. They're even developing robotic bartenders, accelerating automation. Molson Coors has also built an AI-based marketing platform to run personalized ad campaigns. Heineken is far behind its competitors. Simply hiring someone won't close that gap.

Digging deeper, there are clearly "backdoors" that Heineken could exploit with AI. For example, maintaining consistent taste and quality in the brewing process is a crucial task. AI can analyze sensor data to optimize fermentation, temperature, and humidity, and detect anomalies early to reduce defect rates. Also, AI can analyze consumer social media activity and purchasing patterns to help develop new flavors and products. However, realizing these possibilities requires massive investment and data acquisition. It's questionable whether Heineken is willing to do that.

While AI technology itself is rapidly advancing, actually applying it to business and achieving meaningful results is still a difficult task. Many companies invest in AI projects but fail due to lack of data, lack of technology, or resistance from organizational culture. Heineken is likely to fall into these traps as well. Ultimately, the AI Chief's role depends on breaking down data silos, establishing a company-wide AI strategy, and internalizing related technologies.

In conclusion, Heineken's AI Chief appointment is meaningless in itself. What matters is what concrete AI strategy Heineken announces in the future, and what actual results they show. As an investor, there's no reason to put money into Heineken right now. It's not too late to wait and see, and confirm that the facts are accumulating. Right now, all I can say is "wait and see." Frankly, until we see Heineken's next move, it's just noise.
